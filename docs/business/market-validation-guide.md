# Market Validation Guide

This document defines how the Biz team should validate NeTrix before making strong claims in the business plan. The main goal is to test whether the product loop creates enough practical academic value for repeated use.

```text
academic posts -> academic profiles -> AI-assisted recommendations -> academic connections
```

The validation plan should not ask only whether students like the idea. Many platform ideas sound attractive in conversation but fail when users must change behaviour. NeTrix must test whether students will actually post, complete profiles, trust recommendations, and connect with peers.

## 1. Validation Hypotheses

The Biz team should organise validation around a small number of explicit hypotheses.

| Hypothesis | What Must Be True |
| --- | --- |
| Problem hypothesis | Students experience meaningful friction when finding academic information, resources, experience, or relevant peers. |
| Content hypothesis | Students are willing to create or engage with Q&A, Resource, and Experience Sharing posts. |
| Profile hypothesis | Students are willing to build academic profiles if those profiles improve discovery and connection. |
| AI recommendation hypothesis | Students find explainable AI-assisted academic recommendations useful and trustworthy. |
| Connection hypothesis | Students are willing to request, accept, and use academic connections inside a verified campus platform. |
| Network hypothesis | The product becomes more valuable as more students contribute posts and profiles. |

## 2. Interview Plan

The next qualitative research step should be 8 to 12 targeted interviews with Math, Computer Science, and EEE students. The sample should include students from different years and different engagement levels. The goal is not statistical representation; the goal is to understand user reasoning, barriers, language, and behavioural triggers.

Recommended interview topics include current academic information habits, channels used for resources and questions, situations where students failed to find help, attitudes toward sharing academic experience, willingness to maintain a profile, comfort with AI-assisted recommendations, and trust conditions for messaging peers.

Strong interview questions should ask about specific past behaviour rather than abstract preferences. For example, ask "Tell me about the last time you struggled to find a useful academic resource" rather than "Would you use a platform for resources?"

## 3. Seeded-Content Test

Before or during MVP testing, the team should prepare a small but high-quality set of seed posts. An empty feed will make the product feel unconvincing even if the concept is strong.

The seed content should include Q&A posts about common module confusion, Resource posts linking or describing genuinely useful materials, and Experience Sharing posts about coursework, exams, projects, or module selection. The content should be specific to Math, Computer Science, and EEE so that first users immediately recognise relevance.

The purpose of seeded content is not to fake activity. It is to provide enough initial structure for users to understand what good participation looks like.

## 4. MVP Behavioural Metrics

Once the MVP exists, validation should focus on behaviour. The following metrics are more useful than general satisfaction scores:

| Metric | Interpretation |
| --- | --- |
| Profile completion rate | Whether users see enough value to provide academic identity signals. |
| AI profile suggestion acceptance or edit rate | Whether AI-assisted profile refinement feels useful and controllable. |
| Post creation rate | Whether users are willing to contribute public academic signals. |
| Post engagement rate | Whether users read, save, comment on, or otherwise engage with academic content. |
| Recommendation click-through rate | Whether recommended profiles attract attention. |
| Connection request rate | Whether recommendations create enough relevance for action. |
| Acceptance rate | Whether recipients perceive requests as credible and contextually appropriate. |
| Post-acceptance message rate | Whether connections lead to actual academic communication. |
| Returning user rate | Whether value persists beyond curiosity. |

The most important early milestone is not total user count. It is evidence that a small group of students can move through the full loop from posts to profiles to recommendations to connections.

## 5. Validation Milestones

The recommended validation milestones are staged.

| Milestone | Target Outcome |
| --- | --- |
| Discovery validation | Confirm that fragmentation and academic peer discovery are real problems for the first wedge. |
| Concept validation | Confirm that users understand the post-profile-recommendation-connection logic. |
| Content validation | Confirm that seeded posts and early user posts create enough feed relevance. |
| Trust validation | Confirm that students accept campus verification, academic nicknames, and request-based messaging. |
| MVP loop validation | Confirm that users complete the full loop and report practical academic value. |

## 6. Decision Criteria

The Biz team should define what result would justify continuing, pivoting, or narrowing the product. A continuation signal would be repeated user engagement with posts, willingness to complete profiles, meaningful connection requests, and qualitative evidence that recommendations helped users find relevant peers.

A narrowing signal would be strong interest in only one part of the product, such as resources but not connections, or Q&A but not profiles. In that case, the team should consider whether the first wedge or the product loop needs adjustment.

A caution signal would be polite interest but low behavioural commitment. If users like the concept but do not post, complete profiles, or request connections, the business plan should not claim validation.

## 7. Business-Plan Use

The business plan should present validation as an active staged process. It does not need to pretend that all proof already exists. A credible early-stage business plan can be stronger when it clearly states what has been learned, what remains uncertain, and exactly how the next MVP cycle will test the key assumptions.
